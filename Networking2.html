> NETWORKING FUNDAMENTALS: THE PACKET'S JOURNEY

When you send data—like clicking a link or sending an email—your computer doesn't send the whole message at once. Instead, it breaks the data into small chunks called **packets**. These packets then embark on a carefully managed journey to their destination.

On a local network, the primary path for a packet trying to reach the outside world (the Internet) involves three essential steps: the **Gateway**, the **Router**, and the **Firewall**.

Key Definitions

  • Gateway: The **exit door** for all local network traffic. The Gateway address is the IP of the local side of the Router, telling your PC where to send packets destined for the Internet.
  • Router: A specialized device that **connects different networks**. It reads the destination IP in the packet and uses a **routing table** to decide the best path to forward the packet.
  • Firewall: A system that **enforces a set of security rules**. It inspects packets to ensure they are safe and authorized before allowing them to enter or leave the network.
  • Internet: A massive **network of networks** outside your local boundary.
